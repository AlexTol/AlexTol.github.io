What to do During a Drought

Indoor Tips:

Bathroom

  • Avoid flushing the toilet unnecessarily. Dispose of tissue, bugs, and other waste in the trash rather than the toilet.
  • Avoid taking baths and take short showers. Turn on water only to get wet and lather, and then again to rinse off.
  • Avoid letting the water run while brushing your teeth, washing your face or shaving.
  • Place a bucket in the shower to catch excess water for watering plants.

Kitchen

  • Operation automatic dishwashers only when they are fully loaded and use a light wash feature.
  • Hand wash dishes by filling two containers; one with soapy water, and the other with rinse
  • Clean vegetables in a pain filled with water, as opposed to running tap.
  • Store drinking water in the refrigerator.
  • Avoiding wasting water waiting for it to get hot. Use a stove to heat caught water or use it to water plants.
  • Avoiding rinsing dishes before places them in the dishwasher; just remove large particles of food.
  • Avoid using running water to thaw meat or other foods.

Laundry

  • Operate automatic clothes washers only when they are fully loaded or set the water level for the size of your load.

Outdoor Tips:

Car Washing

  • Use a commercial car wash that recycles water.
  • If you wash your own car, use a shut-off nozzle that can be adjusted down to a fine spray on your hose.

Laundry

  • Avoid over water your lawn and water only when needed.
  • A heavy rain eliminates the need for watering up to two weeks.
  • Check the soil moisture levels with a soil probe, spade, or large screwdriver. You don’t need to water if the soil is moist.
  • If your lawn does require watering, do so early in the morning or later in the evening.
  • Check the sprinkler system and prevent water from getting on pavement.
  • Water in several short sessions rather than one long one.
  • Use a broom or blower instead of a hose to clean leaves and debris.
  • Avoid leaving sprinklers or hoses unattended.
  • In extreme drought, allow lawns to die in favor of preserving trees and large shrubs.

Governmental Efforts

California had experienced record dry and warm conditions in 2013, and California had used reservoirs to help meet the water supply demand during this time. California had prepared for the dry season in many ways including the following:

  • 1: California Governor issued an Executive Order in May 2013 to have an orderly and flexible system to allocate water supplies. The orderly allocation process includes various contracts with the state and federal government. This orderly process allow voluntary water transfers more flexible on short-term basis during a serious drought.
  • 2: California Governor directed the California Department of Water Resources to prepare a California Drought Contingency Plan (DCP) in 2008. The plan is the State government’s strategy for managing and developing water resources statewide for current and future generations. The DCP’s purpose is to minimize drought impacts by improving agency coordination, enhancing monitoring and early warning capabilities, and preparedness and recovery programs.
  • 3: Brown signed drought relief legislation worth $687 million in March 2014. It included $25.3 million for food and $21 million for housing for those like farmworkers who are out of work. President Obama announced the federal government would chip in $183 million to the parched state.

During dry periods, each agency and individual, based on their local circumstances, will implement various local actions to help their area get through these dry periods. This includes pumping groundwater, neighbor to neighbor water transfers, internal water management and crop idling. There are three important water management actions that will help the region through dry periods:

  • Flexible Water Transfers Transfer of water is one flexible way to advance the economic, social and environmental sustainability. It is on a short-term basis to work within the current water system.
  • The Value of Surface Storage The existing carryover water storage from 2015 has helped much of the state through intensely dry period in 2016.
  • Sustainable Groundwater Management Groundwater levels and quality in the State are generally excellent according to reports by the Department of Water Resources. This is important because groundwater pumping is increased for all uses during dry periods to make up for surface water supplies that are not available.
